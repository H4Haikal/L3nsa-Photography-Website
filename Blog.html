5 Tips for Perfect Portrait Photography


1. Focus on the Eyes

The eyes are the soul of a portrait, and ensuring they are sharp and well-lit is essential for captivating images. A viewer's gaze is naturally drawn to the subject's eyes, making them the most critical focal point in your composition. To achieve this, use your camera's single-point autofocus mode and position the focus point directly on the eyes. For portraits with a shallow depth of field, such as those shot with wide apertures (e.g., f/1.8 or f/2.8), the eyes need to remain in razor-sharp focus to maintain a professional look. Additionally, if you’re working with a moving subject, continuous autofocus (AI Servo or AF-C mode) can help you track their movement without losing sharpness. For added impact, consider using catchlights—small reflections in the eyes caused by a light source, which add life and vibrancy to your portraits.

2. Use Natural Light for a Softer Look

Natural light offers a softness and warmth that artificial lighting often struggles to replicate, making it ideal for portrait photography. Shooting during the golden hours—shortly after sunrise or before sunset—provides a warm, diffused light that flatters the subject and enhances skin tones. If you’re indoors, position your subject near a large window to take advantage of soft, indirect light. Avoid harsh midday sun, as it can create unflattering shadows and cause your subject to squint. Instead, look for open shade or use a reflector to bounce light onto your subject's face, filling in shadows and creating a more balanced exposure. When natural light isn’t enough, consider supplementing it with a simple external flash or a continuous light source while keeping the natural ambience intact.

3. Choose the Right Background

A cluttered or distracting background can ruin an otherwise great portrait. Always aim for a clean, simple backdrop that keeps the viewer’s focus on the subject. Neutral colors, blurred backgrounds achieved through a shallow depth of field, or natural settings like trees or walls often work best. If you’re shooting in a busy location, like a park or city street, position your subject against an uncluttered background and use a low f-stop (e.g., f/2.8) to blur distractions. Alternatively, consider incorporating elements of the background that add context to the story, such as a chef photographed in a kitchen or a musician with their instrument. The key is to ensure the background complements rather than competes with the subject.

4. Direct Your Subject with Ease

Posing and directing your subject can be one of the most challenging aspects of portrait photography, but it’s also one of the most important. Start by creating a comfortable and relaxed atmosphere—this helps bring out natural expressions. Instead of issuing stiff or formal directions, engage your subject in light conversation to distract them from the camera. Ask for subtle adjustments, like turning their head slightly or tilting their chin, to create flattering angles. Encourage small movements to avoid stiff poses, such as leaning forward, resting a hand on their chin, or shifting their weight from one foot to another. Show them examples of poses if needed, and give positive feedback to boost their confidence. A relaxed subject translates to more authentic and engaging portraits.

5. Experiment with Angles and Composition

The angle from which you shoot can dramatically change the look and feel of your portrait. Experimenting with high, low, and eye-level angles can help you discover what works best for your subject. Shooting from above can make your subject appear smaller or more delicate, while shooting from below can add a sense of power and grandeur. Combine these angles with creative compositions, such as the rule of thirds, to guide the viewer’s eye through the frame. Don’t be afraid to break traditional rules—some of the most striking portraits happen when photographers take risks, like shooting through objects to create foreground blur or placing the subject off-center for a dynamic effect. Incorporating leading lines, natural frames, and intentional cropping can further elevate your portrait photography.

A great portrait captures the soul of the subject, not just their face. Focus on the eyes, use soft natural light, and create an environment where they feel at ease to let their true self shine through.

Haikal Danial
